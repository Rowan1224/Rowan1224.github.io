    R: Corpus Linguistic

    Analysis of Male and Female Speakers’ Word Choices in Public Speeches


    Finding clear and consistent gender differences in language, in general, is not definitive, and the research is largely influenced by the context and technique used to find the difference. Furthermore, the majority of the research has been conducted in texts, with the sample being collected in written form. As a result, I investigated a comparison of male and female presenters' word choices in public speeches such as TED talks. For this investigation, I formulate the following research questions:


    • R1: Are there substantial disparities in word selections between male and female speakers?
    • R2: If they do differ, is there any circumstance in which gender disparities in language are significantly more pronounced?

    I utilized a log odds ratio to assess how word usage varies by gender. Additionally, a MANOVA analysis was performed using the frequency of several categories of words, such as parts of speech (POS), linguistic, psychological, or cognitive terms to find out how male and female speakers utilize words differently. Based on statistical analysis, I discovered that male speakers employ particular types of linguistic, psychological, cognitive, or social words in significantly higher numbers than female speakers.

    NLP: Prompt Engineering

    A Comparison of Prompt Engineering Methods for Checking Verifiability of Online User Comments


    Argumentation mining is a relatively new research area in the field of natural language processing. We have tried to explore this field by looking at the automatic classification of the verifiability of online user comments. We found that the lack of annotated data often present in new research directions and the natural language processing field, in general, can hinder the development of traditional classification techniques. As such, we conducted exploration and comparison of several prompt-based techniques in a few-shot setting on two datasets. Out of all methods, we found that the iterative variant of Pattern Exploiting Training (iPET) performed the best using a RoBERTa base model. Using this method, we achieved macro-averaged F1 scores of 71% and 75% conducting multiclass classification. We achieve somewhat lower results than for our traditional BERT baseline, with which we got F1 scores of 75% and 79% on both datasets.

    This project was an academic group project. I worked on the implementation of the Manual, Auto, and Multi Prompt approaches. Moreover, all the processing and evaluation for these experiments were done by me.

    NLP: Fake News Detection

    BanFakeNews: A Dataset for Detecting Fake News in Bangla


    Observing the damages that can be done by the rapid propagation of fake news in various sectors like politics and finance, automatic identification of fake news using linguistic analysis has drawn the attention of the research community. However, such methods are largely being developed for English where low resource languages remain out of the focus. But the risks spawned by fake and manipulative news are not confined by languages. In this work, we propose an annotated dataset of ≈ 50K news that can be used for building automated fake news detection systems for a low resource language like Bangla. Additionally, we provide an analysis of the dataset and develop a benchmark system with state of the art NLP techniques to identify Bangla fake news. To create this system, we explore traditional linguistic features and neural network based methods. We expect this dataset will be a valuable resource for building technologies to prevent the spreading of fake news and contribute in research with low resource languages.

    NLP: Question Answering

    Question Answering: Fine-tuning and Domain Adaptation with Unlabeled Data


    One of the limitations of the Question Answering (QA) system is the lack of labeled datasets. Though an open-domain question answering system is a potential solution to such a problem, it often comes with a cost of efficiency. Therefore, in our task, we aimed to solve this problem by building a QA system for a specific domain that does not have a labeled dataset. To achieve this, we have fine-tuned pre-trained language models and domain-adapted existing QA models with synthetic QA pairs from the target domain data. We demonstrated our models' capacity for question answering in the experiments by evaluating their EM and F1 scores on the test set. The domain adapted version of Electra has the best achievement with an F1 and EM score of 62.25% and 27.11% respectively. Besides, in the scenario where we only have access to questions and raw dataset, we developed a semantic search technique for constructing a QA system that does not require an explicit context but instead looks for a candidate context inside a raw dataset.

    This project was an academic group project. I worked on the experiments of the Baseline, Albert base, and Albert-SQUaD models. Moreover, I also contributed to the data cleaning and synthetic Synthetic data generation part of this project.

    CV: Image To Text

    Enhancing Handwritten Line Detection Performance with Post-Processing methods


    With the advancement of OCR models, Text Detection from Images has made significant strides in recent years. Even while these models work exceptionally well with digital documents, there is always a potential for improvement in detecting text lines in handwritten documents. Variations in writing style, font size, and image quality have exacerbated this difficulty. There are two typical methods for detecting text lines. Segmenting lines and characters prior to employing a character recognition classifier to map characters to the final text output is one more standard approach. Using an end-to-end model to directly detect text lines from raw image inputs is another method. In this work, utilizing the popular IAM dataset, I converted handwritten line images to text output using an end-to-end approach. In addition, I investigated how the output of the end-to-end model may be further processed to improve text recognition quality. I trained an end-to-end model to recognize line images using a CNN-based model. The integration of spell correction techniques to improve the performance of this end-to-end architecture was my key focus. I have employed three unique post-processing strategies, one of which (brute force) leverages existing vocabulary to replace misspelled words, whereas the other two (edit-distance+LM and NeuSpell) use pre-trained language models to rectify spelling issues. I have concluded that brute force is the most effective tactic and have obtained a Character Error Rate (CER) of 5% using this method.
